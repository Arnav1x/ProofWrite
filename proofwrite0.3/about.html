About ProofWrite · Our Mission

The Pro-Honesty, Anti-AI Homework Tool.

A writing platform built to protect the developmental work that classrooms are meant to do — for the first generation of students growing up alongside ubiquitous AI.

The Problem

A generation raised to outsource thinking.

In recent years, AI has become incredibly efficient — capable of doing work that once took hours or days in a matter of seconds. While revolutionary in certain areas, in the field of education it raises troubling concerns. As teachers are well aware, AI poses a serious academic integrity problem.

For university-aged students, this is concerning but manageable. But what about children in K–5? Older students had the benefit of being raised in an environment where answers were rewarded through learning, thinking, and reasoning. How different would they be if those steps were skipped altogether?

AI is an amazing tool, especially for learning new and confusing concepts. But when students offload all of their thinking into AI, they miss out on a core developmental process. These kids are in the most neuroplastic times of their lives.

For the first time ever, a generation of students is being born into a world of ubiquitous AI. A child as young as five can ask a chatbot to complete their arithmetic homework. If they know how to open a website and take a picture — that's the total amount of thinking and reasoning required for any of their homework.

Their brains should be taught to welcome challenge, not to offload all cognitive adversity to a device that does the thinking for them. ProofWrite aims to solve this dilemma.

What We Are

A homework platform for honesty between students and teachers.

Many of the key learning experiences that prepare children for the future happen outside the classroom. ProofWrite is a homework platform aimed at promoting honesty in that out-of-classroom work.

The platform looks similar to classic text editors like Word and Docs — but it has teachers' interests in mind. Instead of the typical model where teachers assign work, the student completes it on the software of their choice, exports, and submits it to a platform like Canvas, ProofWrite keeps every step internal. The assignment is posted, written, submitted, and graded all in one place.

Key Features

What sets ProofWrite apart.

When a student begins typing, the platform records what is written, at what speed, the gaps between sentences, and every revision. This data is reconstructed into a replayable video of the student's writing session — with speed controls, so teachers can review whether an essay was genuinely written or simply pasted in.

Feature 01
Writing Session Replay
Watch any submission play back word-by-word at the speed it was written, with the option to scrub or speed up. Pastes are visually marked so they're impossible to miss.
Feature 02
Configurable Paste Blocking
Teachers can disable copy-and-paste entirely — with a smart exception for content prefaced by quotes or parentheses, so legitimate citations still work.
Feature 03
Session Metrics
Active time, words-per-minute, tab switches, and pastes — tracked per submission, with thresholds the teacher sets. Science work can allow research tab-outs; book reports cannot.
Feature 04
Teacher Autonomy
Every flag and threshold is adjustable per assignment. ProofWrite surfaces signal — teachers decide what it means.
Our Approach

Color-coded flags, not confidence percentages.

When a student gets flagged, we avoid measurements like percentages. Instead of displaying "this student had a 95% likelihood of academic dishonesty," we use a simple color-coded report:

Green
Nothing out of the ordinary detected. The writing pattern looks authentic.
Yellow
Something questionable was found. Worth a closer look, but not conclusive.
Red
High likelihood of dishonesty. Replay and metadata available for teacher review.

From there, teachers can run diagnostics on each flagged assignment — exporting the replay video and metadata. AI detectors are famously unreliable, and not every flag means cheating occurred. We want teachers to be able to view submissions holistically before making any judgement.
