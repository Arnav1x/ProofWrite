ProofWrite: 
The Pro Honesty, Anti-Ai Homework tool 

Problem
	In recent years, AI has become incredibly efficient. Capable of doing work that once took hours or days in a matter of seconds. While revolutionary in certain areas, in the field of education it raises some troubling concerns. 
I'm sure as many teachers are aware, AI poses a large academic integrity problem. As a sophomore student at UMD, I can attest that it has become commonplace for almost all students to use AI on their assignments. While this is less of an issue for university aged students, what about children k-5? We all had the benefit of being raised in an environment where answers were rewarded through learning, thinking, and reasoning. How different would we be if these steps were skipped altogether?


Mission Statement 
AI is an amazing tool, especially for learning new and confusing concepts, but when students offload all of their thinking into AI they are missing out on a core developmental process. These kids are in the most neuroplastic times of their lives.. What happens when an entire generation is raised to think that whenever they face an issue or roadblock, they can fix it without any critical thinking or problem solving on their end? Their brains should be taught to welcome challenge, not to offload all cognitive adversity to a device that does the thinking for them

For the first time ever, a generation of students are being born into a world of ubiquitous AI. A child as young as 5, can ask a chatbot to complete their simple arithmetic homework for them. If they know how to open a website and take a picture, that’s all the amount of thinking and reasoning, for any of their homework. 

ProofWrite aims to solve this dilemma.

Target audience: K-8 Schools

What we offer / Features
	
	ProofWrite is a homework platform aimed to promote honesty between students and teachers. Many of the key-learning experiences that prepare children for the future happen outside the classroom. 

	The platform appears similar to classic text editors like word and docs. However, it has the interests of teachers in mind. Instead of the typical model, where teachers assign work, the student completes it on the software of their choice, exports, and submits it to a platform like canvas. ProofWrite keeps all of these steps internalized. The assignment is posted on ProofWrite, written on it, submitted on it, and graded on it. 

Key Features: 
	What sets ProofWrite apart, is its Anti cheating/ai features. When a student begins typing, the website begins recording what is written, at what speed, any gaps between sentences, and revisions. The back-end interprets all this data, to create and export a video that shows the student writing their work in real time. Words will appear in the video in the order and speed the student wrote them, with the option of speed control. If needed, teachers can review these videos and see if a student authentically wrote an essay or if it was entirely copy and pasted. 

If opted by the teacher, they can disable copy and pasting entirely. Unless prefaced by an element like “ or ( to accommodate quotes or citations. 

Additionally displays metrics like active time on an assignment, times clicked off to another website. These metrics may flag the student to the teacher, depending on thresholds set by the teacher. If the particular assignment is history and science related, it is expected for students to leave the page and do extra research. However with assignments like book reports, the student is expected to already have the needed knowledge. Teachers have the autonomy to adjust any of these thresholds to what they seem is reasonable. 

When a student gets flagged, we like to avoid measurements like percentages. Instead of displaying “This student had a 95% likelihood of academic dishonesty,” we prefer to use a color-based report. When overlooking submissions for an assignment, a green, yellow, or red is placed next to each submission. Green means that there was nothing out of the ordinary detected, yellow means they found something questionable, and red means there is a high likelihood of dishonesty. From there, teachers have the ability to run the diagnostics of each flagged assignment; exporting the aforementioned video and metadata. From there teachers can take flagged content on a case-by-case basis. AI detectors are famously unreliable and not every flagged assignment means cheating occurred; we want teachers to have the ability to view them holistically, before making judgements.
